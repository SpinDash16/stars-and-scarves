For thirty days this country has been something it swore it would never be: a soccer nation. Your uncle who calls it "kickball" knows the starting eleven. The bartender at your local has opinions about pressing structure. Ten million people learned what a false nine is against their will, and a shocking number of them liked it.

And on July 20, the morning after the final, all of it is up for grabs.

## The moment is bigger than the tournament

Every American soccer generation has its origin story. The 1994 kids became the coaches. The 2002 quarterfinal kids became the bloggers and the podcast hosts. The 2014 Tim Howard kids became the season ticket holders. This summer minted the biggest class of new American soccer fans in history, and they are about to find out what the sport looks like here on a random Tuesday.

That is the whole ballgame. Not the final. The Tuesday after the final.

> A World Cup does not make you a soccer country. What you do with the six months after it does.

## What has to happen now

MLS returns to its regular schedule while the confetti is still on the ground in East Rutherford. The league has maybe three weeks of borrowed attention. The federation has a player pool at peak visibility and a fanbase begging to be organized. The supporters groups, the watch bars, the local clubs, all of them have a once-in-a-lifetime recruiting window.

This journal exists because we think somebody should be keeping score on all of it, loudly, from a barstool in Pensacola. Welcome to Stars and Scarves. The hard part starts now, and we would not miss it for anything.
